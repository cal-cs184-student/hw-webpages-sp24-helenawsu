Tyndall Effect

Summary: The Tyndall effect is the scattering of light by particles in cloudy substances such as dust, causing the light path to become visible. Using ray tracing, we hope to simulate photorealistic tyndall effect with different mediums and light sources.

 

Team members: Helena Su, Anav Mehta, Ge Jin, Jared Basilio

Problem Description

Context: The Tyndall effect is a phenomenon in which light is scattered by particles. It’s observed when the beam of light passes through the colloidal area. To simulate the Tyndall effect accurately, we need to determine the size and concentration of particles in colloidal solution.

Trying to Solve: In the current Homework 3 implementation, the algorithm can only render the light bouncing between surfaces. However, to accurately represent the real-world environments, the simulation must be extended to include the light scattering by small particles suspended in the medium (colloidal solution).

Why it's important: In computer graphics, implementing the Tyndall effect will enable realistic renders of an underwater scene by simulating “god rays” or renders of the atmosphere by creating realistic soft light beams through mist or fog. Adding the support of the Tyndall effect will also be useful in animated movies, video games and VR applications.

Challenge: The key challenge exists in creating a realistic representation of colloidal and modeling the interaction of light with particles of various sizes between. Since the simulation needs to capture the visibility change of the light beam when passing through the medium, we also need to consider the properties of each particle and their effects on our particles in the surrounding environment. Moreover, this can be very computationally expensive and complex as we have to consider every possible interaction between each particle that is affected by the ray and affected by other particles. This creates the problem of finding an alternative solution to these interactions.

Solutions:

a) Extend the ray tracing to enable the interaction between ray and a colloidal medium.

b) We can represent the colloidal solution as a volume within the scene, where each point within the volume can scatter the light. The specific distribution of particles within the volume can be defined through the concentration of the colloidal solution.

c) Add Scattering Simulation: using isotropic scattering models cannot capture the Tyndall effect. The light scattering is dependent on particle size and angular distribution of scatter light. [Larger particles lead to directional light].

d) Optimization of speed: we can use importance sampling, precomputation (precompute the scattering properties), add different levels of details, and add GPU support to accelerate the process.

Goals & Deliverables

What we plan to deliver:

  • Photorealistic images of simple tyndall effect, e.g. visible light paths through a colloidal medium (fog, dust)

  • images showing different color when light shine through (blue light scatters and warm color shine through)

  • Observe differences between different colloidal mediums (mist vs smoke)

What we hope to deliver:

  • Visible light paths under complex light sources

  • Rayleigh effect, similar to Tyndall but smaller particles

  • Rotating camera view

Benchmark:

1. Quality of Rendering: should achieve high fidelity to the Tyndall Effect

a. Visibility of the light paths through the medium

b. Coloration effects due to different particle sizes and densities

c. Detail and Texture: the rendering image should reflect the medium properties

d. Consistency: changes in light source should still maintain the consistency

2. Rendering Time:

        a. Speed: should test in different resolutions & quality. Need to achieve a balance between efficiency and rendering quality

        b. Resources Utilization: GPU / CPU usage
